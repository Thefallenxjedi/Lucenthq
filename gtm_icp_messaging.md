# Internal Memo: Who are we actually selling to?

Look, we have a problem that every early-stage startup has: our product is useful to too many different types of people. 

If we try to build landing pages and run ads that target Enterprise Engineers, Series A Founders, and Product Managers all at the same time, our messaging is going to be incredibly watered down. We need to pick a lane. 

Before we lock in our marketing budget, here is my raw breakdown of the three groups we can target, the pitch for each, and the brutal reality of selling to them.

---

## Target 1: The Enterprise Giants
**The pitch:** "Stop drowning in Sentry noise."

*   **The reality:** These teams have millions of users. When Sentry throws an error, it's a needle in a haystack. Their engineers waste weeks trying to reproduce bugs from vague support tickets. Our pitch to them is that Lucent is the ultimate observability layer—we automatically link their high-level conversion drops directly to the exact session replay and stack trace. 
*   **Why we want them:** The contracts are massive. Once they integrate us, they will never churn.
*   **Why they are terrifying:** The sales cycle will take 6 to 12 months. They will demand SOC2, HIPAA, and custom enterprise security reviews that will drain our engineering resources.

## Target 2: The Fast-Moving Startups (Series A)
**The pitch:** "Move fast without breaking your retention."

*   **The reality:** These guys are shipping code daily to survive. They don't have QA teams; their engineers just build and push. Naturally, they break things, and users churn silently. We sell Lucent to them as an automated QA engineer. We catch the bugs they inevitably ship before the users leave.
*   **Why we want them:** Technical founders make fast purchasing decisions. A CEO or CTO will see the tool, understand the value in 5 minutes, and swipe a credit card.
*   **Why they are terrifying:** Startups fail. Startups run out of money. If we build our entire revenue base on Series A companies, our churn rate is going to be incredibly volatile.

## Target 3: The Frustrated Product Managers
**The pitch:** "Stop begging engineering for data."

*   **The reality:** PMs are in a tough spot. They *know* a checkout flow is confusing, but they have to beg an engineer to write a SQL query to prove it. Or worse, they sit there manually watching 40 hours of PostHog recordings. We give them superpowers. They type what they are looking for, and our AI finds the exact sessions and pre-writes the bug report to hand to engineering. 
*   **Why we want them:** The emotional pain here is huge. PMs desperately want independence.
*   **Why they are terrifying:** PMs usually don't have the company credit card. They have to get the VP of Engineering to approve the budget, which means our tool still has to be loved by the engineers.

---

## The Social Distribution Strategy (Everyone Posts)

Ads take time and money. To get instant, high-trust pipeline, we are going to use ourselves. People buy from people, not faceless company pages. 

*   **My Job (Founder):** I'm building in public. I'm going to talk openly about competing with PostHog and our "AI Credits" pricing model. 
*   **Engineering's Job:** "Show, don't tell." I want our engineers posting screenshots of horrifying React hydration errors and showing how Lucent caught it instantly. Engineers hate marketing, but they love technical teardowns.
*   **Product's Job:** Share the pain. Post about how terrible it is to manually watch session replays and show the "Aha!" moment of having AI just hand you the exact friction points.
*   **The Interns:** Post about the discovery journey. "I just learned how hard QA is, and then I saw this AI tool." These posts go incredibly viral on LinkedIn.

## The Next Step: The Reddit Test
We aren't going to guess which of those three targets is best. We are going to test it with our wallets. 

We will run three separate, cheap ad campaigns on Reddit:
1. `r/SaaS` & `r/startups` (Targeting Series A)
2. `r/ProductManagement` (Targeting PMs)
3. `r/webdev` (Targeting Enterprise/Engineers)

Whichever campaign gets us the cheapest clicks and the most signups is our winner. That becomes our entire focus for the next 6 months.
